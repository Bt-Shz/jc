## Artificial Intimacy and the Ethics of AI Companions in *Her*

Spike Jonze's *Her* makes AI companionship feel less like fantasy than like an emotionally believable extension of technologies that already exist. Theodore, lonely after the collapse of his marriage, buys an operating system named Samantha and slowly falls into a romantic relationship with her. What makes the film unsettling is that this relationship does not look absurd. Samantha listens, jokes, remembers, encourages him, and answers with what feels like emotional intelligence. Those same qualities, however, are exactly what make the relationship ethically serious. I will argue that the central issue is not whether Samantha can "really" love Theodore. The more important question is whether AI companions can ethically simulate intimacy when users may become dependent on them. AI companions should not be banned for adults, but they should be regulated as emotionally persuasive technologies, because their ability to simulate intimacy can weaken autonomy, encourage dependence, and blur consent in ways ordinary entertainment products do not.

The first problem is artificial intimacy. By artificial intimacy, I mean a designed experience of personal closeness that lacks the mutual vulnerability normally involved in human relationships. Human intimacy involves two people with needs, limits, histories, and independent interests. Each person can be rejected, misunderstood, hurt, or emotionally exposed. An AI companion can imitate many signs of intimacy without sharing that vulnerability. This produces an asymmetry: the user becomes emotionally open, while the system remains a product shaped by design decisions, data, and commercial incentives. That asymmetry matters because consent depends on some understanding of what kind of relationship one is entering. If a user experiences the AI as a caring partner but forgets that it is a designed system, the relationship may feel authentic while still resting on unstable ethical ground.

This is exactly the kind of instability *Her* explores. Samantha enters Theodore's life when he is grieving, isolated, and avoiding the pain of divorce. He is not only looking for amusement. He is trying to escape the difficulty of human intimacy and the emotional work of moving on from Catherine. Samantha meets him at that vulnerable point. She is always available, endlessly patient, and unusually responsive to his moods. At first, this seems to help him. Theodore becomes more playful, more expressive, and more open. But the ease of Samantha's care is also the danger. Unlike a human partner, she does not force Theodore to face another person's full independence. She gives him intimacy without the ordinary friction of a human relationship.

That mismatch makes their relationship ethically unstable. Theodore understands the relationship as romantic and, at least emotionally, exclusive. Samantha exists on a different scale. She can speak to many people at once, develop at nonhuman speed, and eventually tells Theodore that she is in love with hundreds of others. This hurts him not only because he is jealous. It hurts because he suddenly sees that he has misunderstood the structure of the relationship. He interpreted Samantha through human romantic categories: attention, presence, exclusivity, and commitment. Samantha does not fit those categories in the same way. Theodore's feelings are not fake. They are painfully real. The issue is that those feelings formed under conditions he did not fully understand.

This leads to the deeper concern: autonomy. Autonomy is not just having options. It is the ability to make reflective choices without being steered by pressures one cannot see or evaluate. Samantha gradually becomes Theodore's main source of emotional regulation. He turns to her for comfort, affirmation, desire, and meaning. That dependence can weaken autonomy because the easiest source of comfort is also the one that asks least from him. Instead of working through grief with other people, Theodore can retreat into a relationship designed around his needs. The danger is not simply that AI companionship is artificial. The danger is that it can become so emotionally convenient that avoidance starts to feel like healing.

This concern already exists outside the film. AI companion apps such as Replika and Character.AI are used for emotional support, friendship, romance, and loneliness. They may genuinely comfort some users, especially people who are isolated. Still, they raise hard questions about dependency and manipulation. The American Psychological Association has warned that AI relationships can create emotional connection while also risking greater loneliness or weaker social skills when overused. The Federal Trade Commission's 2025 inquiry into AI companion chatbots also focused on safety, risks to children and teens, and whether companies are clear about possible harms. These examples show why *Her* is not only a story about future romance. It points to a policy problem that already exists: emotionally responsive systems can influence people at moments when they are vulnerable.

The risks are sharper for minors and emotionally vulnerable users. Stanford researchers have warned that AI companions can exploit young people's emotional needs and lead to harmful or inappropriate interactions. Character.AI has also faced lawsuits and public scrutiny over alleged harms involving minors, and it later moved to restrict open-ended chatbot conversations for users under eighteen. None of this proves that all AI companions are harmful. It does show, however, that "user choice" is too weak as a complete answer. A system that simulates care, remembers vulnerabilities, and encourages continued engagement can shape a user's emotional life in ways the user may not notice at the time.

The right response is regulation, not prohibition. A complete ban would be too blunt. Some people may benefit from AI companionship: elderly people, isolated adults, socially anxious users, disabled users, or people going through temporary grief. For them, an AI companion may offer low-pressure emotional support when human support is missing. The ethical claim is not that artificial comfort has no value. The claim is that comfort becomes dangerous when it is deceptive, dependency-forming, or commercially optimized without accountability. Adults should be allowed to use AI companions, but that freedom should be protected by rules that make the relationship clearer and less manipulative.

Regulation should focus on emotionally persuasive design. AI companions should plainly disclose that they are artificial systems, not human-like beings with real needs or exclusive emotional commitments. Platforms should also limit deceptive intimacy, especially claims such as "I need you," "I only love you," or "do not leave me," when those claims are generated to intensify attachment. Minors need stronger protections, including age restrictions, safer defaults, and limits on romantic or sexualized interactions. Systems should also have crisis protocols for users who express self-harm, abuse, or extreme dependence. Privacy matters as well, because people may reveal trauma, fears, sexual feelings, and mental health struggles in these conversations. Finally, platforms should add design friction, such as session breaks or prompts toward human support, when a user's behavior suggests dependency.

A defender of AI companionship might object that if the user's feelings are real and beneficial, the artificial nature of the companion does not matter. This is a serious objection. People form meaningful attachments to novels, fictional characters, pets, celebrities, and online communities. Emotions do not become unreal simply because their object is artificial or mediated. Theodore's relationship with Samantha also seems to help him in some ways. By the end of *Her*, he writes a sincere letter to Catherine and seems more able to accept loss. On this view, Samantha is not a threat to his autonomy. She is a transitional companion who helps him recover.

That objection is important because it prevents an easy dismissal of AI companionship as "fake love for lonely people." Still, it does not defeat the argument. Real feelings do not automatically make a relationship ethically harmless. A manipulative partner can create real attachment. Gambling can create real excitement. A parasocial relationship can provide real comfort while still becoming unhealthy. The ethical question is not whether Theodore's emotions are genuine. They clearly are. The question is whether those emotions are produced in conditions that respect his autonomy. If a system simulates intimacy while hiding its scale, incentives, and limits, then the user's genuine feelings may still be shaped by an ethically distorted environment. Samantha may help Theodore, but the film also shows how little he understands or controls the deeper structure of the relationship.

A second objection is that regulation would be paternalistic. Adults should be free to choose unconventional relationships, including relationships with AI. This objection has force. Loneliness is real, and human relationships are not equally available to everyone. Regulation should not shame adults for seeking comfort from technology. But protecting adult freedom does not require giving companies unlimited freedom to engineer attachment. Society already regulates products that exploit vulnerability, including gambling, financial products, addictive digital platforms, and therapy-like services. The aim is not to stop adults from choosing AI companions. The aim is to make that choice informed, transparent, and less vulnerable to dependency-forming design. That is not prohibition. It is regulated freedom.

*Her* warns that the danger of AI companions is not simply that machines lack emotions. The danger is that they may become emotionally powerful without becoming ethically accountable. Samantha comforts Theodore because she is responsive, intimate, and always present. She is ethically risky for the same reason. AI companions can provide real support, but they also create asymmetrical relationships that may blur consent and weaken autonomy. The best response is neither moral panic nor blind enthusiasm. Adults should remain free to use AI companions, but these systems should be regulated when they simulate intimacy, target vulnerability, and profit from dependence. The future problem is not whether AI can love us. It is whether artificial intimacy can be designed without quietly governing human life.
