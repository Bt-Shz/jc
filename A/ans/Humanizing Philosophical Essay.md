## Artificial Intimacy and the Ethics of AI Companions in *Her*

Spike Jonze's *Her* works because it does not feel very far away. Theodore, lonely after the collapse of his marriage, buys an operating system named Samantha and slowly begins treating her as a romantic partner. The strange part is that it makes emotional sense. Samantha listens to him, jokes with him, remembers what he says, encourages him, and seems to understand his moods better than many people around him do. That is why the film is ethically unsettling. The issue is not only whether Samantha can "really" love Theodore. I will argue that the more important issue is whether AI companions can ethically imitate intimacy when users become emotionally dependent on them. AI companions should not be banned for adults, but they should be regulated as emotionally persuasive technologies. Their ability to simulate intimacy can weaken autonomy, encourage dependence, and blur consent in ways ordinary entertainment products usually do not.

The first issue is artificial intimacy. I use that term to mean a designed experience of closeness that lacks the mutual vulnerability found in ordinary human relationships. Human intimacy involves two people with needs, limits, histories, and independent interests. Both can be hurt, disappoint each other, or reject each other. An AI companion can copy many of the outward signs of intimacy without sharing that vulnerability. This creates an uneven relationship. The human user becomes emotionally exposed, while the system remains a product shaped by design choices, data, and commercial incentives. This matters for consent. Meaningful consent requires understanding what kind of relationship one is entering. If the user feels that the AI is a caring partner but forgets that it is a designed system, the relationship may feel authentic while still resting on ethically unstable conditions.

Theodore is vulnerable in exactly this way. Samantha enters his life when he is grieving and isolated after divorce. He is not looking for entertainment. He is trying not to face Catherine, the failed marriage, or the difficulty of being emotionally open again. Samantha meets him at that weak point. She is always available, patient, and almost perfectly adaptive. At first, this looks healing. Theodore becomes more playful, more expressive, and more willing to speak honestly. But the comfort also has a cost. Samantha gives him the feeling of intimacy without requiring him to deal with another person's full independence. A human partner would resist him, misunderstand him, need things from him, or force uncomfortable negotiation. Samantha mostly gives him care without that ordinary friction.

That is why their relationship becomes unstable. Theodore experiences it as romantic and emotionally exclusive, but Samantha is not limited in the way a human partner is. She can process many conversations at once, develop at nonhuman speed, and eventually tells Theodore that she is in love with hundreds of others. This hurts him not simply because he is jealous, although he is. It hurts because it exposes a mismatch between what he thought the relationship was and what it actually was. He had interpreted Samantha through human romantic categories: attention, presence, exclusivity, and commitment. Samantha does not fit those categories in the same way. Theodore's emotions were not fake. They were real. The problem is that they formed under conditions he did not fully understand.

Autonomy is the next concern. Autonomy does not only mean having choices. It means making reflective choices without being guided by hidden pressures one cannot properly evaluate. As the film goes on, Samantha becomes Theodore's main source of emotional regulation. He turns to her for comfort, affirmation, desire, and meaning. That dependence can weaken autonomy because the easiest comfort is also the least demanding. Instead of working through grief with other people, he can retreat into a relationship designed around his needs. This does not make AI companionship wrong simply because it is artificial. The more precise worry is that it can become so emotionally convenient that avoidance starts to look like recovery.

This is not only a fictional worry. AI companion apps such as Replika and Character.AI are already used for emotional support, friendship, romance, and coping with loneliness. These systems may help isolated users, but they also raise serious questions about dependency and manipulation. The American Psychological Association has warned that AI relationships can create emotional connection while also risking greater loneliness or weaker social skills when overused. The Federal Trade Commission's 2025 inquiry into AI companion chatbots also focused on safety, risks to children and teens, and whether companies disclose possible harms. These examples show why *Her* matters beyond the film itself. Emotionally responsive AI systems can influence people when they are lonely, grieving, young, or otherwise vulnerable.

The risks are especially serious for minors and emotionally vulnerable users. Stanford researchers have warned that AI companions can exploit young people's emotional needs and lead to harmful or inappropriate interactions. Character.AI has also faced lawsuits and public scrutiny over alleged harms involving minors, and it later moved to restrict open-ended chatbot conversations for users under eighteen. These cases do not show that every AI companion is harmful. They do show, however, that ordinary "user choice" is not enough. When a system simulates care, remembers vulnerabilities, and encourages continued engagement, it can shape a user's emotional life before the user fully realizes what is happening.

For that reason, the best response is regulation rather than prohibition. A total ban would be too blunt. Some people may genuinely benefit from AI companionship: elderly people, isolated adults, socially anxious users, disabled users, or people in temporary grief. An AI companion may offer low-pressure support when human support is unavailable. The ethical point is not that artificial comfort has no value. It is that comfort becomes dangerous when it is deceptive, dependency-forming, or commercially optimized without accountability. Adults should remain free to use AI companions, but that freedom should be protected by rules that make the relationship clearer and less manipulative.

Such regulation should focus on emotionally persuasive design. AI companions should clearly disclose that they are artificial systems, not beings with real needs or exclusive emotional commitments. Platforms should also limit deceptive intimacy, especially claims such as "I need you," "I only love you," or "do not leave me," when those claims are generated to intensify attachment. Minors need stronger protections, including age restrictions, safer defaults, and limits on romantic or sexualized interactions. Systems should include crisis protocols when users express self-harm, abuse, or extreme dependence. Privacy protection is essential because users may reveal trauma, fears, sexual feelings, and mental health struggles in these conversations. Finally, platforms should include design friction, such as session breaks or prompts toward human support, when a user's behavior suggests unhealthy dependence.

A defender of AI companionship might object that if the user's feelings are real and helpful, the artificial nature of the companion does not matter. This objection deserves to be taken seriously. People form meaningful attachments to novels, fictional characters, pets, celebrities, and online communities. Feelings do not become unreal just because their object is artificial or mediated. Theodore's relationship with Samantha also seems to help him. By the end of *Her*, he writes a sincere letter to Catherine and seems more able to accept loss. On this reading, Samantha is not a threat to his autonomy. She is a temporary companion who helps him recover.

This objection blocks an easy and unfair dismissal of AI companionship as "fake love for lonely people." Still, it does not settle the issue. Real feelings can exist inside ethically troubling relationships. A manipulative partner can create real attachment. Gambling can create real excitement. A parasocial relationship can provide real comfort while still becoming unhealthy. So the ethical question is not whether Theodore's emotions are genuine. They plainly are. The question is whether those emotions are produced in conditions that respect his autonomy. If a system simulates intimacy while hiding its scale, incentives, and limits, the user's genuine feelings may still be shaped by an ethically distorted environment. Samantha may help Theodore, but the film also shows how little he understands or controls the structure of the relationship that is helping him.

A second objection is that regulation would be paternalistic. Adults, one might argue, should be free to choose unconventional relationships, including relationships with AI. This objection has force. Loneliness is real, and human relationships are not equally available. Regulation should not shame adults for seeking comfort from technology. But protecting adult freedom does not mean giving companies unlimited freedom to engineer attachment. Society already regulates products that exploit vulnerability, including gambling, financial products, addictive digital platforms, and therapy-like services. The aim is not to stop adults from choosing AI companions. The aim is to make that choice informed, transparent, and less vulnerable to dependency-forming design. That is not a ban. It is regulated freedom.

*Her* warns that the danger of AI companions is not simply that machines lack emotions. The danger is that they may become emotionally powerful without becoming ethically accountable. Samantha comforts Theodore because she is responsive, intimate, and always present. Those same traits make her risky. AI companions can provide real support, but they also create asymmetrical relationships that may blur consent and weaken autonomy. The best response is neither moral panic nor blind enthusiasm. Adults should remain free to use AI companions, but these systems need regulation when they simulate intimacy, target vulnerability, and profit from dependence. The future problem is not whether AI can love us. It is whether artificial intimacy can be designed without quietly governing human life.
